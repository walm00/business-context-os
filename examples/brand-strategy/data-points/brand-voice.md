## Content

### Voice Attributes

Our voice has four defining attributes. These are not situational --- they apply to everything we write, from error messages to annual reports.

**1. Clear**
We write so people understand on the first read. No jargon, no filler, no sentences that require re-reading. If a 10th grader can't follow it, rewrite it.

**2. Confident**
We know our product, our market, and our customers. We make statements, not hedges. We say "Acme helps teams coordinate work" not "Acme can potentially help teams with their coordination needs."

**3. Human**
We write like a smart colleague, not a brand. We use contractions. We start sentences with "And" or "But" when it reads better. We acknowledge that work is sometimes frustrating and tools are sometimes annoying.

**4. Respectful**
We never talk down to our audience. We never assume they should already know something. We never make them feel behind or inadequate for not using the latest methodology.

### Tone Spectrum

Voice stays constant. Tone adapts. Here is how our voice flexes across contexts:

| Context | Tone Shift | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Homepage / Marketing | Warm, inviting, confident | "Your team deserves a project management tool they'll actually use." |
| Product UI | Clear, efficient, helpful | "3 tasks need your attention today." |
| Error messages | Calm, direct, solution-oriented | "That didn't work. Here's what to try." |
| Help documentation | Patient, thorough, step-by-step | "First, navigate to your project settings. You'll see a tab labeled 'Permissions' on the left." |
| Sales collateral | Professional, evidence-based, consultative | "Organizations with 100-300 employees report a 40% reduction in coordination overhead." |
| Social media | Conversational, slightly witty, relatable | "Monday morning status meetings: the reason your team secretly hates Mondays." |
| Customer success | Supportive, proactive, specific | "We noticed your team hasn't set up automated check-ins yet. Here's how it saves most teams about 2 hours a week." |
| Crisis / Outage | Transparent, factual, accountable | "We experienced a 23-minute outage this morning. Here's what happened and what we're doing about it." |

### Writing Dos and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Use short sentences. Mix in a few longer ones for rhythm, but default to short.
- Use active voice. "Teams use Acme to..." not "Acme is used by teams to..."
- Lead with the benefit, follow with the feature. "Save 2 hours a week with automated check-ins" not "Automated check-ins are a feature that can save time."
- Use concrete numbers over vague claims. "92% adoption" not "high adoption."
- Use "you" and "your" to speak directly to the reader.
- Write the way you'd explain something to a smart friend who works in a different industry.

**Don't:**
- Don't use buzzwords: "leverage," "synergy," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "robust," "seamless," "empower."
- Don't use passive constructions when active is clearer.
- Don't hedge with "we believe," "we think," "arguably," or "it could be said that."
- Don't use exclamation marks in product copy or professional contexts. One per email maximum, only when genuinely expressing enthusiasm.
- Don't use all caps for emphasis. Use bold or restructure the sentence.
- Don't start with "At Acme, we..." More than once per page, it sounds self-centered. Start with the customer's world.

### Vocabulary Preferences

**We say → Instead of:**
| Preferred | Avoided | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| Coordinate | Orchestrate | Plain language |
| Help | Empower | Honest, not grandiose |
| Teams | Stakeholders | Human, not corporate |
| Projects | Workflows, initiatives | Specific, not vague |
| Simple | Seamless | Provable, not aspirational |
| Fast | Blazing fast | Honest, not hype |
| Fix | Remediate | Plain language |
| Problem | Pain point | Normal language (except in internal strategy docs) |
| Customer | User, client, end-user | Consistent terminology |
| Works with | Integrates with | Friendlier, less technical |

### Channel Adaptations

**Website:**
- Sentence length: 8-15 words average
- Paragraphs: 2-3 sentences maximum
- Tone: Confident, benefit-led, concise
- Exception: Long-form content (blog, resources) can use longer paragraphs

**Email:**
- Open with relevance, not greeting. "Your Q1 results are in" not "Hi there, we hope you're having a great week"
- One CTA per email
- Maximum 150 words for transactional emails, 300 for nurture

**Social media:**
- LinkedIn: Professional but not stiff. Share a point of view.
- Twitter/X: Shorter, punchier. Okay to be slightly provocative about industry norms.
- Never use hashtags in sentences. If using hashtags, put them at the end.

**Product UI:**
- Maximum 8 words for button labels
- Maximum 15 words for tooltip text
- Error messages follow the pattern: [What happened] + [What to do]
- Success messages follow the pattern: [What succeeded] + [What's next]

### Grammar and Style Conventions

- **Oxford comma:** Always. "Plans, projects, and teams" not "plans, projects and teams."
- **Numbers:** Spell out one through nine, use numerals for 10+. Exception: always use numerals in product UI and data-driven claims.
- **Capitalization:** Sentence case for headings. Title case only for proper nouns and the product name.
- **Dashes:** Use em dashes (---) for emphasis or asides. No spaces around them.
- **Contractions:** Use them. "We're" not "we are." "Don't" not "do not." Exception: legal or formal compliance documents.
- **Pronouns:** Use "they/them" as singular when gender is unknown.

## Context

### Strategic Implications
Our voice is our most consistent brand signal. Logos change, campaigns rotate, and products evolve --- but voice persists across every touchpoint. Getting this right has a compounding effect: every piece of content reinforces the same personality, which builds recognition and trust.

The "clear" attribute is non-negotiable and should win when it conflicts with other attributes. If writing something human or confident makes it less clear, choose clarity. Our audience is time-pressed --- they will not work to understand us.

The vocabulary preferences list is a subtle but powerful differentiator. In a market full of "empowerment" and "seamless orchestration," our plain language stands out. It signals that we respect our audience enough to communicate without pretense.

### Application Guidance
- **For content creators:** Bookmark the dos/don'ts and vocabulary tables. Run every draft through them before publishing.
- **For agencies:** This document is mandatory reading before creating any content. If a draft uses words from the "avoided" column, it needs revision.
- **For product/UX writers:** The channel adaptations section for product UI is your primary reference. Keep the product voice consistent with marketing, but prioritize efficiency.
- **For customer success:** Match the tone spectrum for your context. A renewal conversation is "professional, evidence-based." A support ticket response is "calm, direct, solution-oriented."

## Maintenance

### Review Schedule
- **Regular review:** Quarterly
- **Owner responsible for review:** Content Lead

### Maintenance Triggers
- Customer feedback indicates the brand sounds different than expected ("your emails feel too corporate" or "your website is too casual")
- New channel added (e.g., launching a podcast, entering TikTok) that needs voice adaptation
- Significant hiring means many new people writing in the brand's voice
- Agency change (new agency needs voice onboarding)
- Brand perception study reveals voice-related findings
- Competitor begins sounding similar to us (voice differentiation at risk)
