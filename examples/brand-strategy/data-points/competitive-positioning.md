## Content

### Market Position

**Where we play:** Mid-market project management --- the space between simple task managers and enterprise portfolio management suites.

**How we win:** By being the tool that mid-market teams actually adopt and keep using, not the tool that looks great in a demo and gets abandoned in 90 days. Our advantage is sustained adoption, not feature count.

**Positioning territory we own:** "Project management that respects how mid-market teams actually work."

This territory is deliberately distinct from:
- "The most powerful PM tool" (enterprise players own that)
- "The simplest way to manage tasks" (lightweight tools own that)
- "AI-powered project intelligence" (emerging players are fighting over that)

### Competitive Landscape

#### Tier 1: Direct Competitors (compete for the same buyers)

**Competitor A --- Monday.com (Mid-Market Plans)**
- Position: "Work OS" --- broad platform for managing any workflow
- Overlap: Their mid-market pricing tier targets the same company size
- Their strength: Brand recognition, visual appeal, breadth of use cases
- Their weakness: Complexity creep --- they keep adding features for enterprise, making mid-market experience heavier
- Our advantage against them: We are purpose-built for project coordination at mid-market scale, not a general-purpose work platform stretched to fit

**Competitor B --- Teamwork.com**
- Position: "Project management for client work"
- Overlap: Strong in professional services, our #1 segment
- Their strength: Deep focus on client-facing project management, time tracking, budgeting
- Their weakness: Narrow focus limits appeal outside professional services
- Our advantage against them: We serve all mid-market project types, not just client work. Teams that run internal and external projects prefer one tool.

**Competitor C --- ClickUp (Business Tier)**
- Position: "One app to replace them all"
- Overlap: Aggressive mid-market pricing, strong product-led growth
- Their strength: Feature density, low price point, strong developer community
- Their weakness: Overwhelming for non-technical users; reliability and performance concerns at scale
- Our advantage against them: We optimize for the operations leader who needs things to work reliably, not the power user who wants maximum customization

#### Tier 2: Adjacent Competitors (compete at the edges)

**Enterprise (above us):** Smartsheet, Planview, Microsoft Project --- too complex and expensive for our audience, but some mid-market companies default to them because they think "enterprise = professional"

**Lightweight (below us):** Asana Free/Starter, Trello, Notion --- good for small teams, but coordination breaks down at 50+ people. We win when companies outgrow these.

### Differentiation Strategy

**Primary differentiator:** Adoption-first design. We measure success not by feature breadth but by the percentage of team members who actively use the tool after 90 days. Our design decisions, onboarding, and pricing all optimize for sustained team adoption.

**Supporting differentiators:**
1. **Mid-market native:** Built specifically for 50-500 person companies. We did not start as a small-team tool that added enterprise features, and we did not start as an enterprise tool that created a "lite" tier.
2. **Opinionated simplicity:** We deliberately limit configuration options to prevent the "blank canvas paralysis" that kills adoption of flexible tools. New teams are productive on day one because we provide structured defaults.
3. **Coordination over tracking:** Competitors focus on tracking tasks (what's done, what's overdue). We focus on coordination (who needs what from whom, what's blocking progress, where are the handoff points). This is the real pain at mid-market scale.

### Competitive Response Guidelines

**When a prospect asks about a competitor:**
- Acknowledge the competitor respectfully. Never disparage.
- Redirect to our positioning territory: "They are a strong [category] tool. Where we differ is [differentiation]."
- If the competitor is a better fit, say so. The "honest partnership" value applies here.

**When a competitor is mentioned in content or press:**
- Do not engage in feature comparison wars publicly.
- Respond with customer stories that demonstrate our differentiation, not feature lists.

**Phrases we use:**
- "Built for mid-market teams, not adapted from enterprise"
- "We optimize for adoption, not feature count"
- "Coordination, not just tracking"

**Phrases we never use:**
- "We're better than [competitor]"
- "They can't do what we do"
- "All-in-one" or "replace all your tools"

## Context

### Strategic Implications
Our positioning territory ("project management that respects how mid-market teams actually work") is defensible because it requires a specific product philosophy, not just features. Competitors cannot copy this by adding a feature --- they would need to redesign their entire approach to mid-market.

The biggest positioning risk is getting pulled upmarket. As our largest customers grow past 500 employees, they will request enterprise features. Every enterprise feature we add dilutes our mid-market positioning. This tension must be managed deliberately, not reactively.

The adoption-first differentiator is measurable and provable. We should invest in making our adoption metrics public (anonymized and aggregated) as a proof point. "92% average team adoption after 90 days" is more powerful than any feature comparison.

### Application Guidance
- **For sales:** Lead with the adoption story, not the feature list. When a prospect asks "can you do X?" respond with "yes, and here's how it affects adoption" rather than just "yes."
- **For marketing:** Position against the category (complex enterprise tools and insufficient lightweight tools), not against specific competitors. We win by reframing the decision, not by winning a feature comparison.
- **For product:** The "opinionated simplicity" differentiator means every feature request should be evaluated with "does this make the tool simpler for 80% of users, even if it limits the 20% who want maximum flexibility?"
