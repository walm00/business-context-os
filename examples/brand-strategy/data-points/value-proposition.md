## Content

### Core Value Proposition

**For mid-market operations leaders who need to coordinate work across multiple teams:** Acme is the project management platform that your team will actually adopt and keep using, delivering visible coordination improvements within the first month --- without the complexity, training overhead, or cost of enterprise alternatives.

### Benefit Hierarchy

#### Primary Benefits (What buyers care about most)

**1. Team-wide adoption without training programs**
Most project management tools require dedicated training sessions, certification programs, or at minimum a "champion" who spends weeks configuring the system. Acme is productive on day one because it mirrors how mid-market teams already coordinate --- through check-ins, shared visibility, and lightweight status updates. The result: 92% average active user rate after 90 days, compared to the industry average of roughly 40-60% for comparable tools.

**2. Coordination visibility without overhead**
Operations leaders currently piece together project status from Slack threads, email chains, spreadsheet trackers, and weekly status meetings. Acme consolidates coordination into one view: who is working on what, where handoffs are stuck, and which projects need attention. Customers report eliminating an average of 3 recurring status meetings per week within the first month.

**3. Mid-market appropriate cost and complexity**
Enterprise project management tools cost $30-80 per user per month and require 2-6 months of implementation. Lightweight tools cost less but break down at 50+ people. Acme is priced for mid-market budgets and productive within the first week, not the first quarter.

#### Secondary Benefits (Important but not the primary reason to buy)

**4. Cross-department project visibility**
When marketing, product, and operations all manage projects in the same tool, leadership gets a real picture of capacity, conflicts, and progress --- without building custom reports or hiring a PMO.

**5. Structured defaults that reduce decision fatigue**
Instead of presenting a blank canvas and asking teams to design their own workflow, Acme provides opinionated project templates based on how mid-market teams actually work. Teams can customize later, but they start productive.

**6. Scalable without re-implementation**
As companies grow from 50 to 500 people, Acme grows with them. No re-implementation, no migration, no "you've outgrown the starter tier" forced upgrades. The same tool works at 80 people and 400 people.

#### Tertiary Benefits (Nice to have, not decision-drivers)

**7. Integration with existing tools**
Connects with Slack, Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, and common mid-market tools. Teams do not have to leave their communication tools to use Acme.

**8. Reporting without a data team**
Built-in dashboards show project health, team capacity, and delivery trends without requiring custom queries, BI tools, or a dedicated analyst.

### ROI Narrative

**The cost of the status quo:**
Mid-market operations leaders spend an estimated 8-12 hours per week on project coordination activities: status meetings, Slack follow-ups, spreadsheet updates, and email chains. For a team of 5 managers, that is 40-60 hours per week --- the equivalent of one full-time employee doing nothing but coordination.

**The Acme outcome:**
Our customers report a 40% reduction in time spent on project coordination within the first quarter. For a 200-person company, that translates to roughly 20 reclaimed hours per week across the management team --- hours that go back into strategic work, team development, or simply going home on time.

## Context

### Strategic Implications
The benefit hierarchy reflects a deliberate strategic choice: we lead with adoption, not features. This is counterintuitive in a market where competitors lead with capability ("look at all the things our tool can do"). Our bet is that mid-market buyers have been burned by tools their teams refused to use, and adoption confidence is the most powerful purchase motivator.

The ROI narrative targets the CFO or finance approver who needs to justify the expense. The "one full-time employee doing nothing but coordination" framing is more compelling than abstract percentage improvements because it connects to headcount --- something every mid-market leader understands viscerally.

The feature-to-benefit mapping deliberately uses customer language, not product language. "I see where every project stands without asking anyone" is how a customer describes the coordination dashboard. Using their words in our value narrative makes the benefit feel real rather than marketed.

### Application Guidance
- **For product marketing:** Use the benefit hierarchy to structure all value-focused content. Primary benefits should appear in every major touchpoint. Secondary benefits appear in deeper content (webinars, case studies, detailed pages). Tertiary benefits are supporting details, not headlines.
- **For sales:** The ROI narrative is your closing tool. Calculate the specific numbers for each prospect based on their team size and current coordination overhead.
- **For product:** The feature-to-benefit mapping should be reviewed before every launch. If a new feature does not have a clear benefit in customer language, it is not ready to announce.
- **For customer success:** Use quantifiable claims as benchmarks during onboarding. "Most teams see X by week 4" sets expectations and gives customers a way to measure their own progress.
